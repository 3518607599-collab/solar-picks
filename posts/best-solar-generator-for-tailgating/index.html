What Power Do You Need for Tailgating?

Before you buy, figure out what you're actually running. Here's a realistic breakdown:

Basic Tailgate (Phones, Music, Lights)

  • Phone charging (10W × 4 phones × 2h = 80Wh)
  • Bluetooth speaker (10W × 6h = 60Wh)
  • LED string lights (10W × 5h = 50Wh)
  • Total: ~190Wh/day

Mid-Level Tailgate (Add a TV and Blender)

  • Everything above, plus:
  • 32" LED TV (50W × 5h = 250Wh)
  • Blender for margaritas (400W × 0.5h = 200Wh)
  • Total: ~640Wh/day

Full Game Day Setup (The Works)

  • Everything above, plus:
  • Electric griddle (1,200W × 1h = 1,200Wh)
  • Portable fan (40W × 5h = 200Wh)
  • Mini projector (100W × 3h = 300Wh)
  • Extra phone/tablet charging (100Wh)
  • Total: ~2,440Wh/day

Most tailgaters fall into the mid-level category. If you're running a griddle or electric smoker, you'll need a bigger unit with a higher output rating.

Tailgating Power Tips

Here's what we've learned from real game days:

Charge the night before. Don't rely on solar panels at the tailgate — you won't have time or ideal angle. Top off from a wall outlet the night before and use solar as a bonus during the day.

Know your wattage. Check the label on your appliances. A blender might say 400W but pull 800W on startup (surge). Make sure your generator's surge rating covers it.

Bring a power strip. Most generators have 2-4 AC outlets. A small surge protector lets you plug in more devices without constantly swapping cables.

Keep it in the shade. Solar generators have operating temperature limits. Sitting in direct sun on a hot parking lot can cause overheating. Keep it under your canopy or in the shade of your vehicle.

Don't drain it to zero. LiFePO4 batteries handle deep discharge better than older lithium-ion, but you'll extend the life of your generator by keeping it above 20% whenever possible. Our solar generator maintenance guide covers battery care in detail.

Can You Actually Recharge at the Tailgate?

Technically yes, but practically it's limited. A 200W solar panel in ideal conditions adds about 150-180Wh per hour. On a partly cloudy day in a parking lot with the panel propped at a less-than-ideal angle, expect more like 80-120Wh per hour.

That means a 200W panel could recoup about 500-700Wh over a 5-6 hour tailgate — enough to offset a TV and phone charging, but not enough to replace what a griddle uses. Solar panels are great for extending your runtime, not replacing a full charge.

Solar vs Gas Generator for Tailgating

If you're still on the fence between solar and gas, here's the short version: solar wins for tailgating. Here's why:

  • No noise. Gas generators roar. Your neighbors in the parking lot will hate you. Solar generators are silent.
  • No fumes. You can use a solar generator inside your tent or under your canopy. Never do that with gas.
  • No fuel to carry. One less thing to pack and worry about.
  • No maintenance. No oil changes, no spark plugs, no winterizing.

The only advantage of gas is raw sustained power — if you're running a 2,000W appliance for hours on end, gas is cheaper per watt-hour. For a typical tailgate, solar is the clear winner. Our solar generator vs gas generator guide dives deeper into this comparison.
